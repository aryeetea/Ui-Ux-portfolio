Case Studies

How I actually think through things.

These go deeper into each project. The problem, what I did, and why.

We built Dear Stranger around one question: what would it look like to make slowness the point?

My sister moved to the United States, and even though she was surrounded by people every single day, she still could not find a way to make real connections. Social media feels performative. Messaging apps feel like tasks. Everything moves too fast. I brought that idea to my friends, and we built Dear Stranger together.

This was a collaborative class project, and none of us worked in strict roles. We all contributed to design decisions, feature priorities, user flows, and code. We aligned on one core idea: if the product was going to help people connect more honestly, then slowness had to be part of the design, not something users had to fight for.

The Problem

There is a very specific kind of loneliness that most platforms do not address: being somewhere new, surrounded by strangers, with no real way in. Most digital tools are built for people who already have a social life, not for people trying to build one slowly.

How We Worked

We collaborated across everything equally. We tested the star map discovery experience together, iterated on it through each other's feedback, and let the visual direction grow out of shared conversations about what the platform should feel like.

Design Philosophy

The dark void background made the universe between people feel infinite. Gold accents brought warmth into that darkness. Cinzel gave labels a timeless quality, while Cormorant Garamond made the letters feel intimate and handwritten. Each user got an animated hub so no one felt like just another profile.

Core Flow

Discover on the star map, visit a hub, write a letter, and wait for a reply. The pause is part of the experience, not a flaw in it.

Outcome

Dear Stranger launched at dearstranger.xyz and picked up organic traffic from Google, Discord, and LinkedIn without any marketing. In the first week it reached 37 page views, with real users creating hubs and writing letters.

This site had to look legit the second you opened it.

I worked on this for a real healthcare training business. It couldn't just look pretty — people needed to actually trust the info and be able to find what they needed without getting lost.

I treated trust like a design requirement. I made it easy to find course info and how to get in touch, but kept the UI clean and easy to read. I also set it up with WordPress so the client can edit stuff easily herself now that it’s live.

The Problem

People in healthcare don't want a messy site. It had to be super clear and easy to use from the start.

Finding Things

I made it easy to find classes and certs so people don't have to go searching around for 10 minutes.

The Layout

I kept it simple. Clean fonts, good spacing, and nothing extra that didn't need to be there.

Outcome

The final site works great for the students and the client can edit stuff without needing me. It just feels professional.

I wanted it to feel like the culture but also just work like a normal food app.

Gobɛ Hemaa was more than just ordering food. I wanted to design something that felt warm and cultural but could still handle the serious stuff like payments and order tracking without any glitches.

I used a lot of bright colors and familiar patterns to make it feel inviting. But underneath that, I had to keep the code disciplined so people could actually browse, pay, and get their food without getting confused.

The Problem

A lot of food apps look exactly the same. I wanted this one to stand out and feel like the brand without making it hard to use.

Design

I used warmth and color to show personality, but kept the layout simple so you can still order fast.

Payments

The payment part had to work with Paystack and Hubtel. I had to design for when things worked and also for when they didn't, so no one loses their money.

Outcome

It feels vibrant and real to the business, but it's also super reliable where it counts.
