How I think through product, interface, and implementation.

We built Dear Stranger around one question: what would it look like to make slowness the point?

My sister moved to the United States, and even though she was surrounded by people every single day, she still could not find a way to make real connections. Social media feels performative. Messaging apps feel like tasks. Everything moves too fast. I brought that idea to my friends, and we built Dear Stranger together.

This was a collaborative class project, and none of us worked in strict roles. We all contributed to design decisions, feature priorities, user flows, and code. We aligned on one core idea: if the product was going to help people connect more honestly, then slowness had to be part of the design, not something users had to fight for.

The Problem

There is a very specific kind of loneliness that most platforms do not address: being somewhere new, surrounded by strangers, with no real way in. Most digital tools are built for people who already have a social life, not for people trying to build one slowly.

How We Worked

We collaborated across everything equally. We tested the star map discovery experience together, iterated on it through each other's feedback, and let the visual direction grow out of shared conversations about what the platform should feel like.

Design Philosophy

The dark void background made the universe between people feel infinite. Gold accents brought warmth into that darkness. Cinzel gave labels a timeless quality, while Cormorant Garamond made the letters feel intimate and handwritten. Each user got an animated hub so no one felt like just another profile.

Core Flow

Discover on the star map, visit a hub, write a letter, and wait for a reply. The pause is part of the experience, not a flaw in it.

Outcome

Dear Stranger launched at dearstranger.xyz and picked up organic traffic from Google, Discord, and LinkedIn without any marketing. In the first week it reached 37 page views, with real users creating hubs and writing letters.

A healthcare training company needed a website that would convince prospective students to enroll.

Before designing anything, I researched the industry thoroughly and studied every competitor site I could find. The patterns were consistent: poor color usage, weak hierarchy, crowded layouts, and no clear sense of trust or professionalism.

Because people making career decisions need confidence immediately, I treated clarity as the core design requirement. The layout was rebuilt around distinct sections, generous spacing, readable typography, and straightforward decision paths that made the site feel more credible and easier to use.

The Problem

Every sterile processing school website I found had the same problems: poor color usage, no clear sections, everything crammed onto one page, and no visual hierarchy. They made an important decision feel harder than it should.

Research Findings

Competitor sites lacked visual hierarchy, used color inconsistently, had poor information architecture, and communicated little credibility or professionalism. These were all signals I needed to correct.

Design Decisions

I used clear sections, clean typography, generous spacing, a prominent application form with autosave, a testimonial carousel, FAQ accordion, and a Google Maps embed for local trust and discoverability.

Core Flow

Land, learn about the program, read testimonials, and apply. The design was meant to make the next step feel obvious and reassuring.

Outcome

The site is live at dascstraining.com and actively serving real users. The client can manage content independently through WordPress, and the project was delivered on time with a clean handoff.

Built WithReact, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS, React Router, Web3Forms, WordPress, Vercel
Role UI/UX Designer and Frontend Developer
Project Type Freelance Client Project

My cousin in Ghana started a food business and wanted me to invest, so I offered something better and built her a platform.

Gobɛ Hemaa means Queen of Food in Twi, and that meaning shaped the entire product direction. This was not just about putting a menu online. It was about giving the business a digital home that felt culturally connected, visually warm, and trustworthy enough to support real transactions.

The challenge was building something that felt like hers while also handling the realities of e-commerce. Existing food ordering platforms in Ghana either did not support local payment methods or felt completely disconnected from Ghanaian culture.

The Problem

My cousin had no online presence. The available food ordering options either lacked local payment support or felt generic and culturally disconnected. She needed something that actually felt like hers.

Design Decisions

I used warmth and color to reflect the culture and brand, but kept the layout simple so ordering stayed fast and frictionless. Cultural expression should never slow down the transaction.

Payment Challenge

Integrating Paystack, Flutterwave, and Hubtel required designing checkout flows for Mobile Money, cash on delivery, and the unique success and failure states of each payment method.

Core Flow

Browse menu, add to cart, create account, checkout, and confirm order. The experience had to feel familiar and trustworthy from start to finish.

Outcome

The product is live at gob3hemaa.vercel.app. It is a real platform built for a real business, designed with cultural intention and shipped from scratch.
