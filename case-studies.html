Case Studies

How I think through the product, the design, and the build.

These are the fuller stories behind the projects, what the problem was, how I approached it, and what made the final result work.

Dear Stranger started with one question, what if slowness was the whole point?

My sister moved to the United States, and even though she was around people every day, she still couldn't find real connection. Social media can feel kind of for show. Messaging apps can feel like work. Everything moves too fast. I brought that idea to my friends, and we built Dear Stranger together.

This was a collaborative class project, and none of us stayed in strict roles. We all touched design decisions, feature priorities, user flows, and code. The main thing we agreed on was simple. If we wanted people to connect more honestly, the product had to make space for that.

The Problem

There's a very specific kind of loneliness most platforms don't really deal with. You're somewhere new, surrounded by strangers, and there's no real way in. Most digital products are made for people who already have a social life, not people trying to build one slowly.

How We Worked

We pretty much worked on everything together. We tested the star map discovery flow as a group, gave each other feedback, and let the visual direction grow naturally from conversations about how the platform should feel.

Design Philosophy

The dark space background made the distance between people feel huge. The gold added warmth. Cinzel gave the labels a timeless feel, and Cormorant Garamond made the letters feel more personal, almost handwritten. We also gave each person an animated hub so they wouldn't just feel like another profile.

Core Flow

You discover someone on the star map, visit their hub, write a letter, and wait for a reply. That pause is part of the experience. Honestly, that is the point.

Outcome

Dear Stranger launched at dearstranger.xyz and got organic traffic from Google, Discord, and LinkedIn without any marketing. In the first week it reached 37 page views, and real users were already creating hubs and writing letters.

A healthcare training company needed a website that could help students feel ready to enroll.

Before I designed anything, I spent time researching the industry and looking through competitor sites. Honestly, the same problems kept showing up, bad color choices, weak hierarchy, crowded pages, and not much trust.

Since people making career decisions need confidence right away, I treated clarity like the main requirement. I rebuilt the layout around clear sections, better spacing, readable type, and paths that made the next step feel obvious.

The Problem

Almost every sterile processing school site I found had the same issues, bad color use, weak sections, too much on one page, and barely any hierarchy. They made an important decision feel more confusing than it needed to be.

Research Findings

The competitor sites had weak hierarchy, inconsistent color, messy information structure, and not much credibility. Those were exactly the things I knew I had to fix.

Design Decisions

I used clear sections, clean type, more breathing room, a visible application form with autosave, testimonials, an FAQ accordion, and a Google Maps embed so the business felt more real and easier to trust.

Core Flow

You land on the site, learn about the program, read testimonials, and apply. I wanted the next step to feel obvious and reassuring.

Outcome

The site is live at dascstraining.com and being used by real people. The client can manage content through WordPress, and I delivered it on time with a clean handoff.

Built WithReact, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS, React Router, Web3Forms, WordPress, Vercel
Role UI/UX Designer and Frontend Developer
Project Type Freelance Client Project

My cousin in Ghana started a food business and wanted me to invest, so I did something better and built her a platform.

Gobɛ Hemaa means Queen of Food in Twi, and that shaped the whole direction. This wasn't just about putting a menu online. It was about giving the business a digital home that felt warm, cultural, and trustworthy enough for real orders.

The challenge was making something that actually felt like hers while still handling the real side of e-commerce. A lot of food platforms in Ghana either didn't support local payment methods or just felt disconnected from the culture.

The Problem

My cousin didn't have an online presence. The food ordering options out there either didn't support local payments or felt too generic and disconnected from the culture. She needed something that really felt like her business.

Design Decisions

I used warmth and color to reflect the culture and the brand, but I kept the layout simple so ordering stayed easy. I wanted the personality to be there without slowing anything down.

Payment Challenge

Getting Paystack, Flutterwave, and Hubtel working meant designing checkout flows for Mobile Money, cash on delivery, and all the different success and failure states that come with each payment method.

Core Flow

You browse the menu, add to cart, create an account, check out, and confirm the order. I wanted the whole thing to feel familiar and trustworthy from start to finish.

Outcome

The product is live at gob3hemaa.vercel.app. It's a real platform for a real business, built from scratch with culture in mind.
