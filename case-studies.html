Case Studies

How I think through the product, the design, and the build.

Each case study documents the actual problem, the constraints, the decisions I made, and the evidence I have that the solution worked.

Dear Stranger asked a product question most social platforms avoid: what if slowness was not friction, but the feature?

The idea came from watching how isolation can persist even when someone is constantly online and surrounded by people. Most digital products optimize for speed, visibility, and response loops. That makes them good at activity, but not always good at sincerity.

We built Dear Stranger as a collaborative class project, but my contribution was specific. I owned the experience of discovering people, entering their personal space, and writing to them. My job was to make a vulnerable interaction feel safe enough to try.

Context

Project type: collaborative class project. Product type: social platform. User need: a lower-pressure way to connect with strangers without performing for an audience or managing instant replies. The project was exploratory, but we treated it like a real product problem rather than a visual concept. That meant the experience had to be coherent enough for actual people to move through it, not just interesting enough to present in class.

Problem

The core problem was not just loneliness. It was the fact that existing products make first contact feel exposed, noisy, and disposable. Feeds reward visibility. Messaging apps reward speed. Both create pressure to be clever, fast, and socially fluent. If the goal was thoughtful connection, the interface could not behave like a feed or a chat app because those patterns would have pushed users into performance instead of reflection.

What I Owned

I led the UX for the star map discovery flow, the structure of each user's hub, and the letter-writing experience. I also defined much of the visual direction for those flows, including how discovery should feel, how profiles should be framed, and how the act of writing should slow the user down instead of rushing them forward. In practical terms, I was responsible for the parts of the product that determine whether a stranger feels approachable, whether the interface feels intimate or impersonal, and whether writing feels meaningful enough to complete.

Constraint

The biggest constraint was conceptual. We were building a social product, but the usual social patterns would have broken the premise. If we made discovery too efficient or communication too immediate, the product would drift back into the same performative behavior it was supposed to resist. There was also a presentation challenge: the product needed to feel emotionally distinct without becoming confusing or overly abstract. If the metaphor became more important than the usability, the idea would collapse under its own aesthetic.

Key Design Decisions

I used the star map as the discovery model because it made exploration feel deliberate rather than feed-driven. That choice changed the emotional posture of the product immediately. Instead of scrolling past people, users move toward them. I designed each person's hub to feel like entering a space, not opening a profile card, because that shift adds emotional weight and slows judgment. I kept letter writing asynchronous because waiting was part of the value proposition, not a system weakness. Visually, I used darkness to represent distance and warm gold to prevent the interface from feeling emotionally sterile. I also leaned into editorial typography so the writing flow would feel reflective rather than chat-like.

Why Those Decisions Matter

Every choice had the same job: reduce performance pressure. The product needed to signal that users did not have to be witty, immediate, or highly visible to participate. That is why the flow is slower, the visuals are quieter, and the writing interface feels more ceremonial than transactional. The design rationale only works if the emotional promise is consistent across the whole journey. If even one part of the flow suddenly behaved like a standard social app, the experience would feel incoherent and the trust the interface was trying to build would disappear.

Tradeoff

The main tradeoff was accessibility versus novelty. A more conventional interaction pattern would have been easier to understand immediately, but it would also have undermined the premise. I accepted a slightly less familiar interaction model because the emotional behavior of the product mattered more than short-term pattern recognition. The job was not to mimic existing social products better. It was to create a different emotional outcome.

Outcome

Dear Stranger shipped as a live product at dearstranger.xyz. The best evidence of success is that the concept moved beyond classwork into actual behavior: users created hubs, explored the network, and sent letters. For this project, the real outcome was proving that an unconventional social interaction model could be implemented as a functioning product and used by real people. That matters because many student projects stop at the concept stage. This one had to hold up as an interactive system with enough coherence that strangers could understand what to do and why the product was different.

What Would Strengthen This Case Study

To make this story even stronger for recruiters, I should add the early discovery sketches, the hub wireframes, and at least one iteration that shows how the writing flow changed over time. The product idea is distinctive; the next step is making the process evidence equally visible.

DAS Sterile Training was not a branding exercise. It was a trust problem disguised as a website problem.

The client needed a public-facing site that could help prospective students understand the program and feel confident enough to apply. In this category, confusion is expensive. When someone is evaluating career training, weak structure quickly reads as weak credibility.

I approached the project as an enrollment experience, not just a brochure site. The goal was to remove uncertainty and make the next step feel justified.

Context

Project type: freelance client work. Product type: healthcare training website. Business need: communicate legitimacy, explain the offer clearly, and support applications from prospective students. This project sits in a category where design quality directly affects perceived credibility. People are not casually browsing. They are evaluating whether a program feels serious enough to trust with their time, money, and career direction.

Problem

Most competitor sites in this space were visually cluttered and structurally weak. Important information was buried, sections blended together, and the sites gave too few trust signals for such a serious decision. If DAS looked similar, users would have to work too hard to feel confident. The problem was not only visual inconsistency. It was that poor information structure made the business seem less established than it really was.

What I Did First

I audited competing sterile processing training sites to understand the category baseline. The recurring issues were weak hierarchy, inconsistent styling, and poor information flow. Key details about the program, enrollment process, and business legitimacy were often scattered or visually flattened. That audit gave me a clear design target: make credibility immediate and next steps obvious. Instead of trying to look dramatically different from the category, I focused on being clearer, more trustworthy, and easier to act on.

Key Design Decisions

I built stronger sectioning and visual hierarchy so visitors could scan tuition, program details, and next steps without cognitive overload. I added testimonials, FAQ content, and a location embed because prospective students do not just need information, they need reassurance that the business is real and established. I also made the application path more visible so interest could turn into action without users hunting for where to go next. The site was intentionally more restrained than flashy because in this context visual drama would not have improved trust. Clarity, spacing, and predictable structure were more valuable than stylistic experimentation.

Why Those Decisions Matter

In a healthcare education context, clarity is not cosmetic. It affects whether a business seems competent. The hierarchy, spacing, trust signals, and content structure all work together to answer a user's silent questions: Is this legitimate? Can I trust this program? What do I do next? When users do not have to fight the layout for basic understanding, they can spend their energy evaluating the offer itself. That shift is the real UX improvement.

Tradeoff

The main tradeoff was between expressive branding and institutional clarity. I chose not to make the interface overly stylized because the primary job of the site was to reduce doubt, not showcase visual personality. In another industry I might have pushed the visual system further, but here restraint was part of the solution.

Core Flow

The intended sequence is simple: understand the program, verify legitimacy, then apply. I designed the page order so each section earns the next level of commitment instead of asking for action too early. Users first get orientation, then supporting proof, then a clear path to application. That sequence matters because asking for action before establishing trust would create drop-off through hesitation, even if the form itself worked perfectly.

Outcome

The site launched at dascstraining.com with client approval and became the live public face of the training center. That matters because this was not a speculative redesign. A real business accepted the work, uses it publicly, manages content through WordPress, and has reported no post-launch issues. The strongest proof here is adoption: the client trusted the final product enough to make it their live presence, which is a much more credible outcome than saying the design simply looked better than before.

What Would Strengthen This Case Study

This case study still needs artifact-level proof: screenshots from the competitor audit, low-fidelity layout exploration, and a before-versus-after view of the information hierarchy. Those visuals would make the strategic improvement much easier to see in one glance.

Gobɛ Hemaa was not just a restaurant site. It was a real ordering system for a business that needed local relevance and operational credibility.

My cousin's business needed more than visibility. She needed a platform customers could actually order from, trust, and pay through using familiar methods. A generic e-commerce template would not have solved that.

I treated the project as a product design problem with local constraints: culturally specific branding, straightforward ordering, and payment flows that match how customers in Ghana actually complete transactions.

Context

Project type: personal client-style build for a family business. Product type: food ordering platform. Business need: move from no online ordering presence to a trustworthy storefront that supports real transactions. Unlike a showcase site, this product had to do operational work. The interface needed to carry brand identity, but it also needed to support ordering behavior without introducing doubt at checkout.

Problem

The business had no online ordering presence, and the available alternatives either felt culturally generic or failed to support the payment behaviors customers actually use. The challenge was to create a platform that felt both local and dependable. If the site felt too generic, it would miss the identity of the brand. If it felt expressive but operationally weak, customers would hesitate at the point of payment.

Key Design Decisions

I used warm tones, food-forward imagery, and culturally grounded branding so the site felt tied to the actual business instead of looking imported from a generic template. At the same time, I kept the menu, cart, and checkout patterns familiar because ordering food depends on trust and speed more than novelty. The visual system had to express personality without making users second-guess the transaction. In other words, the brand needed to feel culturally specific, but the mechanics of ordering needed to feel immediately legible.

Operational Constraint

The hardest part of the project was not the homepage. It was payment. Supporting Paystack, Flutterwave, and Hubtel meant designing around different provider behaviors, redirect patterns, success states, failure states, and user expectations. If checkout broke trust, the rest of the design would not matter. This is where the project became a real product design exercise instead of a visual one, because the interaction details at payment determine whether the business can actually use the platform.

Why Those Decisions Matter

Food ordering products succeed when users feel oriented and confident. That is why I kept the browsing and cart experience conventional while putting the brand distinctiveness into tone, color, and imagery. The design supports local identity without forcing users to learn a new ordering pattern. That balance is important because users should notice the brand, not struggle with the flow.

Tradeoff

The main tradeoff was between expressive visual identity and checkout simplicity. I deliberately kept the transactional moments more conventional than the homepage because the cost of confusion rises sharply once money is involved. A more experimental checkout might have felt distinctive, but it would have weakened trust where the product most needed to feel reliable.

Core Flow

Users browse the menu, add items to cart, create an account, select a payment path, and confirm the order. I designed the flow to minimize uncertainty at checkout because payment is the point where trust becomes a real business outcome. The goal was not just to make the interface usable. It was to make the entire transaction feel expected, supported, and credible from menu to confirmation.

Outcome

Gobɛ Hemaa launched as a live storefront for a real business at gob3hemaa.vercel.app. The strongest evidence here is functional: the platform supports real orders, checkout works across three payment providers, and the product launched with no reported bugs after release. That outcome is important because the value of the project lives in operational reliability. The most compelling proof is not that the site looks warm or polished. It is that a real business can use it to take orders and accept payment through locally relevant methods.

What Would Strengthen This Case Study

The next improvement is visual proof of the system thinking behind the work: checkout flow maps, payment-state wireframes, and iterations showing how I balanced cultural expression with transaction clarity.
